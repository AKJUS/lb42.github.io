Part Three : The Nineties

The 1990s: Things could only get better

  • Widespread discontent over the poll tax, mad cow disease, economic recession, and the resignation of several ministers finally brought down Mrs Thatcher. She was replaced by John Major, who did at least sign the Maastricht agreement which created the EU ...
  • For years Belinda had in her window a sign saying “Stop the Poll Tax” and another saying “Free Nelson Mandela”, The first happened in 1992, the second in 1994. The sign saying “This is not here” didnt seem to have any effect.
  • In academia, everyone was talking about something cool called the World Wide Web: I went to a conference in Warsaw in 1992 at which a French man assured a room full of skeptical hackers from across Europe that the Web would very soon be commercialised. The number of organisations with a Web presence went from a few hundred in 1990 to many thousands in 1999.
  • On Mayday 1997, after 18 years of Tory misrule, the Labour party (well, Tony Blair) won a General Election with the biggest majority held by any government since 1935.
  • In November 1994 Eurostar began services through the Channel Tunnel.
  • The Good Friday Agreement was signed on 10th April 1998, bringing power sharing and at least the semblance of an end to Northern Ireland’s ‘Troubles’.
  • This was the age of Brit Pop (Radiohead, Oasis, Blur, Pulp, Massive Attack, The Spice Girls ect ect )

On the other hand:

  • At the beginning of the decade, we were suckered into joining the first Gulf War.
  • At its end, July 1998, a little bit of the British Empire called Hong Kong was handed back to the Chinese (it seemed a good idea at the time; it wasn't).
  • Princess Diana died in a car crash at the end of August 1997, leading to widespread public lamentation and media frenzy, but not (curiously) the demise of the monarchy.

Some say that the true meaning of "TEI" was "Travel Expenses Indefinitely". Lodgers

The top floor of no 110 had two bedrooms, one of which we split in two. For most of the year there was a steady flow of student lodgers sleeping there, usually eating dinner with us, and always helping to keep the mortgage under some degree of control.

The lodgers were not university students, but a mixture of A-level re-takers, lost souls striving to scrape through entrance exams, abandoned children of expat poshos from across the world. A few became family friends, but only a few. The Japanese were the most polite and they always came bearing gifts.

In March 1997, we organized a mass expedition to the Trattorio da Olga in Cereglio, just over the hill from Guy's house in Liserna. This was to celebrate Guy's 50th (and mine and Lilette's too), though we returned frequently thereafter, on any excuse.

In July 1998, everyone went to Mauritius, without me (I stayed in Oxford to run the first ever Text Encoding Summer School, closely followed by TALC conference)

And in August 1999, at Allen Renear's invitation, we took Elizabeth on her first transatlantic adventure, via Iceland. We explored Boston, Providence, and Newport RI. We also had a few days cat-sitting in Greenwich Village and visited MOMA.
